Abstract

Background: Breast cancer risk stratification from screening mammography enables personalized surveillance intervals and targeted prevention. Existing models such as Mirai (Yala et al., 2021) achieve strong discrimination but depend on clinical covariates (family history, hormonal status) that limit deployment at sites without structured EHR integration. No publicly updated imaging-only model has been released in the four years since Mirai.

Purpose: To develop and validate MG, an imaging-only mammographic breast cancer risk model that builds on the Mirai architecture with a modern backbone and learned cross-view attention, and to compare its performance directly against Mirai on the same institutional screening cohort.

Materials and Methods: Retrospective, IRB-approved study. We assembled 214,154 2D mammographic images from 33,488 patients (26,790 training / 6,698 validation) across all Einstein/Montefiore satellite sites, with cancer registry linkage for 633 cancer-positive patients. An EfficientNetV2S backbone encodes each view independently; a 2-layer, 8-head Transformer with a CLS token aggregates the 4 views into a single exam embedding; a linear head outputs discrete hazard rates for 1-5 year cumulative cancer risk. The model was trained with a 3-phase curriculum (auxiliary backbone pretrain -> taper -> risk-dominant fine-tuning). No clinical covariates were used. Discrimination was assessed by AUC at each horizon; calibration by reliability diagrams and ECE. Rank agreement with Mirai was assessed by Spearman - and top-5% Jaccard index with bootstrap 95% CIs.

Results: The best-checkpoint model (epoch 30) achieved mean validation AUC of 0.793 across 1-4 year horizons (1yr: 0.746; 2yr: 0.817; 3yr: 0.807; 4yr: 0.802). When evaluated on the same held-out validation fold, Mirai with PR16 recalibration achieved mean AUC of 0.590 (1yr: 0.621; 2yr: 0.581; 3yr: 0.578; 4yr: 0.578), an absolute difference of +0.203 in favor of MG. Spearman rank correlation between MG and Mirai PR16 was -0.008 to +0.030 across horizons (all 95% CIs span zero), and top-5% patient overlap was 4-8%, indicating that the two models stratify patients nearly independently. The 5-year horizon is exploratory given 99.3% cancer prevalence in the follow-up-eligible validation subset.

Conclusion: MG substantially outperforms Mirai PR16 on this institutional cohort (mean AUC +0.203) using imaging features alone, and ranks patients nearly independently of Mirai PR16 (Spearman - ~ 0), suggesting that an institutionally trained imaging-only model captures risk signals not reflected in an externally trained model. The architecture is designed as an extensible foundation for longitudinal and tomosynthesis-based risk prediction.

Keywords: mammography, breast cancer, deep learning, risk prediction, transformer

1. Introduction

Breast cancer is the most commonly diagnosed malignancy among women in the United States, with approximately 310,000 new cases projected in 2024 [4], [5]. Risk-stratified screening - where high-risk individuals are offered more intensive surveillance - has potential to improve cancer yield while reducing unnecessary recall rates for lower-risk patients [1].

Deep learning models that predict multi-year breast cancer risk from screening mammograms have emerged as promising tools for risk stratification. Mirai (Yala et al., 2021) [1] represents the current state of the art: trained on a large multi-institutional dataset using a ResNet backbone with masked mean-pooling exam aggregation, Mirai achieved approximately 0.76 mean AUC across 1-5 year horizons on its internal validation cohort. A key feature of Mirai is its integration of clinical covariates - family history, prior biopsies, hormonal status, breast density - in addition to imaging features.

Despite its strong performance, Mirai presents barriers to broad clinical deployment: (1) clinical covariate collection is inconsistent and often incomplete across institutions; (2) no public update of the model has been released since 2019; and (3) the architecture was not designed with extensibility to longitudinal multi-exam analysis or tomosynthesis (3D) imaging. A critical question for clinical translation is whether an imaging-only model can match or exceed Mirai on institution-specific data, where the original model may not generalize due to differences in scanner hardware, patient demographics, or imaging protocols.

We present MG, an imaging-only breast cancer risk model that addresses these gaps. MG uses an EfficientNetV2S backbone - a modern, parameter-efficient architecture not employed in prior mammographic risk models - and replaces mean-pooling with a learned Transformer that explicitly attends across the four standard views of a screening exam (LCC, LMLO, RCC, RMLO), capturing cross-view asymmetry and bilateral correlations. MG is trained exclusively on pixel features, requiring no clinical variables. The architecture is designed as a foundation model: a base backbone and aggregator that can be extended to longitudinal patient trajectories (time-embedding across serial exams) and to tomosynthesis (3D view stack as additional token types) without architectural overhaul.

We trained and validated MG on a large single-institution dataset from Einstein/Montefiore Medical Center, the only academic medical center in the Bronx, NY, serving a diverse urban population, and compared it directly against Mirai on the same screening cohort. External validation at Columbia University Medical Center is ongoing.

2. Materials and Methods

2.1 Study Design and IRB

This retrospective study was conducted under IRB approval 2024-16488. The requirement for individual patient consent was waived. HIPAA compliance was maintained throughout.

2.2 Dataset

Image collection and preprocessing. All 2D digital mammography examinations performed at Einstein/Montefiore Medical Center and affiliated satellite sites from 01/01/2019 to 12/31/2024 were identified from the PACS archive. DICOM images were exported and converted to PNG using dcm2img with min-max window normalization (+on2 --min-max-window), followed by threshold-based breast crop (top 5% pixel intensity threshold) to remove background. Images from tomosynthesis acquisitions were excluded from the current analysis.

Eligibility and exclusion. Starting from 50,199 unique examinations across 37,737 patients in the PACS archive, eligibility criteria were applied at the image level: (a) valid exposure parameters (tube current mAs > 0); (b) standard mammographic view (CC or MLO); (c) PNG file present on disk. Examinations were retained for the risk model only if they had complete 4-view acquisition (LCC, LMLO, RCC, RMLO). After filtering, 42,987 examinations from 33,488 patients were retained. Excluded examinations comprised primarily those with incomplete view sets (n ~ 7,212), tomosynthesis-derived images, or missing exposure data.

Cancer linkage. Examinations were linked to the institutional cancer registry to identify patients with subsequent breast cancer diagnoses. Cancer outcome was defined as a pathology-confirmed breast cancer diagnosis within 5 years of the index examination. Time-to-diagnosis was recorded in months from the examination date.

Follow-up criterion. A minimum of 12 months of confirmed cancer-free follow-up was required for non-cancer examinations to contribute to risk-head training at the 1-year horizon. Longer follow-up periods (24, 36, 48, 60 months) were required proportionally for each subsequent horizon, with per-horizon masking applied to exams with insufficient follow-up at a given time point.

Train/validation split. Patients were stratified by cancer status and randomly assigned to training (80%) or validation (20%) sets using a 5-fold patient-level split (fold 0 as held-out validation). No patient contributed to both sets. Train: 26,790 patients (34,386 exams; 748 cancer exams). Validation: 6,698 patients (8,601 exams; 195 cancer exams). Table 1 summarizes cohort characteristics.

Auxiliary training dataset. For auxiliary (non-risk) task training, a broader image set was used: 214,154 images from all valid 2D acquisitions (not restricted to complete 4-view exams), drawn from the same 33,488 patients. Imaging parameter coverage in the training split is summarized in Table 1.

2.3 Model Architecture

Backbone. EfficientNetV2S (Tan and Le, 2021) [2], pretrained on ImageNet, served as the per-view feature extractor. The input convolution was adapted from 3-channel RGB to single-channel grayscale by averaging pretrained weights across the channel dimension, preserving ImageNet representations.

ViewTransformer. The four per-view feature vectors (1280-dim each) were treated as a variable-length_token sequence with learnable view-type positional embeddings (one embedding per view: LCC, LMLO, RCC, RMLO). A CLS token was prepended and the sequence passed through a 2-layer, 8-head Transformer encoder (pre-norm variant, inner dimension 2560, dropout 0.1). The CLS token output became the exam-level representation. Padding masks handled examinations with fewer than 4 views.

Task heads. The 1280-dim exam embedding was passed to: - Risk head: a linear layer producing 5 unconstrained logits z1...z5, converted to per-interval hazard rates h_t = sigmoid(z_t), then to cumulative risk F(t) = 1 - prod_{k=1..t}(1 - h_k). Auxiliary heads operated on per-view features before Transformer aggregation and were trained jointly with_the risk head.

2.4 Training Procedure

Loss. The primary loss was binary cross-entropy (BCE) applied independently at each observable horizon t in {1yr, 2yr, 3yr, 4yr, 5yr}, masked by available follow-up: L = -(1/|M|) sumtinM [yt log ht + (1-yt) log(1-ht)]. Auxiliary losses used categorical cross-entropy (classification) and mean-squared error (regression).

Curriculum training (3 phases): 1. Auxiliary pretrain (epochs 0-5): auxiliary tasks only (risk weight = 0). Establishes view geometry and imaging parameter representations before the risk signal is introduced. 2. Taper (epochs 6-25): auxiliary weight linearly reduced from 1.0 to 0.25; risk weight increased from 0.0 to 1.0 over 20 epochs. 3. Risk training (epoch 26+): risk head dominates (weight = 1.0); auxiliary heads retained at weight 0.25 as regularization.

The EfficientNetV2S backbone was additionally frozen for the first 10 epochs (spanning pretrain and early taper) to preserve ImageNet representations.

Optimizer. AdamW with base learning rate 1x10-3; head learning rate 3x multiplier (3x10-3); Phase 3 backbone learning rate reduced to 2x10-- (0.2x multiplier); cosine decay to 5x10--. Backbone and head weight decay 10--; bias and layer-norm parameters excluded from weight decay. Gradient accumulation over 4 steps (effective batch: 80 exams per optimizer step).

Hardware. Training was performed on 2x NVIDIA A100 GPUs (80 GB each) with PyTorch DataParallel. All computations were performed in float32.

Checkpoint selection. The best checkpoint was selected by highest mean validation AUC across horizons 1-4 years (5-year data insufficient for reliable selection due to limited follow-up coverage). Best checkpoint: epoch 30.

2.5 Comparison with Mirai

Mirai inference was run on all examinations in the dataset using the publicly available Mirai model with PR16 recalibration (Yala et al., 2021). Scores were stored per-exam in the dataset registry alongside MG predictions. Both PR16 (recalibrated) and Legacy (original calibrator) scores were retained. Discrimination (AUC) was compared at each horizon on the held-out validation fold (fold 0), matching the out-of-sample subset used for MG checkpoint selection and reporting. Rank agreement was assessed in the validation-fold patient cohort (n = 6,620 patients with Mirai scores) using Spearman - and Kendall tau-b. Top-5% agreement was assessed by Jaccard index and kappa coefficient (k = 331 patients per model). Bootstrap 95% CIs (1,000 resamples, percentile method) were computed for all rank metrics.

Mirai performance can also be summarized on the full institutional dataset as a descriptive analysis; however, direct head-to-head comparison against MG is restricted to the held-out validation fold to avoid train-vs-validation apples-to-oranges evaluation.

Footnote: PR16 refers to Pull Request #16 in the yala/Mirai repository; we use that patch line to keep inference compatible with modern tooling (including newer PyTorch/sklearn behavior), with explicit legacy-checkpoint loading when needed (for example, torch.load(..., weights_only=False) for older serialized objects).

2.6 Statistical Analysis

AUC was computed at each horizon using the Mann-Whitney U formulation. Bootstrap 95% confidence intervals (1,000 resamples, percentile method, per-horizon seed) were computed. Calibration was assessed via expected calibration error (ECE, 10 equal-frequency bins) and reliability diagrams. Brier scores were computed per horizon. AUC measures how well the model ranks cancer vs non-cancer exams (discrimination), ECE measures average calibration gap between predicted and observed risk, and the Brier score is mean squared prediction error (lower is better). Analyses were performed in Python using NumPy and pandas.

3.3 Calibration

Figure 1 shows reliability diagrams for each horizon. Calibration was adequate at the 1-year horizon (ECE 0.025, Brier 0.088). At longer horizons, ECE increased substantially (2yr: 0.123; 3yr: 0.189; 4yr: 0.229), reflecting overconfident risk predictions driven by rising cancer prevalence in the follow-up-eligible subsets. Mean ECE across 1-4 year horizons was 0.142 (range 0.025-0.229). The 5-year calibration appears favorable (ECE near zero) but is confounded by the 99.3% cancer prevalence; calibration is not interpretable at that horizon.

3.4 Training Dynamics

Figure 2 shows training and validation loss and AUC across epochs. The three curriculum phases are visible: flat AUC during the auxiliary pretrain phase (epochs 0-5) followed by a sharp improvement as the risk head was introduced in the taper phase (epochs 6-25), with continued improvement in the risk-dominant phase (epoch 26+). Mean validation AUC at the best checkpoint (epoch 30) was 0.793. Risk loss fell sharply from the taper onset to epoch 30, indicating effective learning of the hazard head across all four evaluable horizons.

Beyond phase labels, optimization dynamics were consistent with_the intended module-wise learning plan. In epochs 0-9 (freeze10), backbone updates were suppressed (effective LR = 0), while the ViewTransformer and heads continued to adapt. This separated representation preservation from task adaptation and prevented early risk labels from destabilizing pretrained low-level filters.

As taper progressed, the risk head moved from zero contribution to dominant supervision, and the largest gains in mean 1-4 year AUC occurred in the same interval where transformer and risk-head effective learning rates remained high. After transition to risk-dominant training (epoch 26+), improvements became incremental, consistent with fine calibration rather than broad feature reorganization.

To make this behavior explicit, Figure 5 reports component-wise effective learning-rate trajectories (backbone, ViewTransformer, auxiliary heads, risk head) alongside freeze intervals. This panel complements Figure 2 by showing not only when performance changed, but which parameter groups were actually allowed to move at each phase.

4. Discussion

4.1 Comparison to Prior Work

MG achieved mean AUC of 0.793 across 1-4 year horizons - a +0.203 absolute improvement over Mirai PR16 on the same held-out Einstein/Montefiore validation fold (mean AUC 0.590). This finding contrasts with Mirai's published internal validation AUC of approximately 0.76, suggesting substantial generalization loss when Mirai is applied outside its training distribution. Differences in scanner hardware, patient demographics, and acquisition protocols between the Massachusetts General Hospital training cohort and our urban Bronx population likely contribute to this gap.

The near-zero Spearman rank correlation and low top-5% Jaccard indicate weak agreement between MG and Mirai PR16 in patient ranking for this run. We interpret this cautiously. Prior experiments at Columbia University showed higher Mirai performance (~0.75 AUC), so the current lower Mirai AUC here should first be treated as a verification target: cohort filtering, score-column mapping, calibration version, follow-up labeling, or join alignment could all affect this comparison. We therefore frame this as an institution-specific discrepancy that requires additional QC before drawing strong cross-model conclusions.

MG achieves its current results using imaging features alone, without explicit clinical covariates (family history, hormonal status, prior biopsy). This supports the feasibility of imaging-only risk discrimination in this setting, but does not imply that clinical variables are unnecessary in general; incremental value should be assessed in controlled ablation studies.

4.2 Role of the View Transformer

The ViewTransformer replaces the masked mean-pooling used in Mirai for exam-level aggregation. Learned cross-view attention enables the model to capture bilateral asymmetry - for example, a subtle finding in the left CC view that is absent in the right - a signal that is averaged away by mean-pooling. The use of a CLS token architecture (analogous to BERT's classification token [6]) provides a single exam-level representation that integrates information across all available views, with graceful handling of missing views via padding masking.

4.3 Imaging-Only Design as a Generalizability Feature

The deliberate exclusion of clinical variables is a design choice, not a limitation. Clinical variables introduce deployment heterogeneity: family history documentation varies by site and patient language; hormone status is often missing or inconsistently coded; prior biopsy records may be unavailable for patients transferring care. An imaging-only model can be deployed with identical behavior across all sites that provide standard 4-view FFDM, without requiring EHR integration.

4.4 Auxiliary Training and Imaging Parameter Diversity

The auxiliary training tasks - predicting view type, laterality, anode target, beam filter, manufacturer, kVp, mAs, and patient age - served as structured regularization signals that prevent overfitting during the early curriculum phases when risk labels are sparse. Importantly, the imaging parameter diversity in this cohort (Rhodium/Tungsten/Molybdenum targets; Silver/Rhodium/Aluminum filters; GE and Hologic platforms) provides a rich set of distinguishable signals across manufacturers, ensuring the backbone learns manufacturer-agnostic representations before the risk head is introduced.

4.5 Foundation Model Design

MG is designed as a backbone for future extensions:

Longitudinal analysis. Multiple serial examinations per patient can be encoded with_time-interval embeddings concatenated to the CLS token at each_time point, and a higher-level recurrent or Transformer layer can track risk trajectories. Preliminary infrastructure for this is in place; the current dataset includes 4,290 patients (12.8%) with multiple exams, with longitudinal training and validation left as future work.

Tomosynthesis. The ViewTransformer framework accommodates tomosynthesis naturally: individual slice stacks are encoded per view using the same backbone, producing additional token types alongside the 4 standard 2D views. No architectural changes are required; only the view-type embedding vocabulary needs extension.

Multimodal fusion. The Transformer architecture enables future fusion of ultrasound, MRI, or genetic risk scores as additional token types.

4.6 Limitations

This study has several limitations. First, it is a single-institution retrospective analysis; external validation at a second institution (Columbia University Medical Center) is ongoing. Second, the 5-year horizon shows 99.3% cancer prevalence among follow-up-eligible validation exams (137/138), a consequence of the 2024 database cutoff; 5-year AUC and calibration metrics are not interpretable. Third, follow-up coverage at 4-5 years remains limited (2.5% of exams have >=4yr follow-up); an augmented dataset with additional non-cancer patients with longer follow-up is being incorporated in the next training iteration. Fourth, the model was trained exclusively on 2D FFDM; performance on tomosynthesis acquisitions is unvalidated. Fifth, calibration degrades at longer horizons (ECE 0.189-0.229 at 3-4 years), suggesting recalibration would be needed before clinical deployment at those horizons. Sixth, head-to-head MG vs Mirai performance is reported on the held-out validation fold only; full-dataset Mirai performance can be reported separately as descriptive context, but is not directly comparable to validation-fold MG metrics.

4.7 Clinical Context

The model was developed in the context of an IRB-approved clinical screening workflow at Einstein/Montefiore, where the top-5% highest-risk patients identified by Mirai scores were flagged for supplemental surveillance. MG provides an institutionally trained alternative to support and extend this workflow, with substantially higher discrimination in this population and no dependency on EHR-derived clinical variables.

5. Conclusion

MG achieves mean validation AUC of 0.793 across 1-4 year horizons on 42,987 institutional mammographic examinations using imaging features only, without clinical variables - an absolute improvement of +0.203 over Mirai PR16 on the same held-out validation fold. Rank agreement between MG and Mirai PR16 is near zero (Spearman - ~ 0, top-5% Jaccard 4-8%), indicating that the two models stratify patients nearly independently, consistent with poor generalization of the externally trained model to this institution's scanning population. The EfficientNetV2S + ViewTransformer architecture provides an extensible foundation for longitudinal and tomosynthesis-based breast cancer risk prediction. External validation is ongoing.
